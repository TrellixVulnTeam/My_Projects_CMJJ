Is Social Media changing the English Language?

OMG! Too many buzzwords in the English Dictionary! The English language is constantly changing to accommodate developments in technology and culture. While some words appear quickly and then fade away, others become so commonplace that they immediately become permanent fixtures in our language. In the recent past, Social media has played an important role in determining how dictionaries learn about potential new words. Instead of waiting for new words to gradually make their way through traditional channels, modern dictionaries use social media to monitor new words. Let's take a look at how the English Language is getting influenced, or rather, evolved by social media trends.

      The Word cloud presented above consists of the most popular words in tweets during the years 2009, 2016 and 2017. Later in this article, we will witness that these words have actually managed to find inclusions in the official English dictionaries.

      “Selfie”, “hashtag”, “unfriend” and “YOLO” are a few of the social media-inspired suggestions that have been considered for dictionary inclusion. With the need for quick and succinct language and communications online, full verb phrases have become common acronyms that are now used in everyday settings and not just online. Phrases such as “rolling on the floor laughing” or “talk to you later” are quickly changed to “ROFL” or “TTYL”. These short acronyms creep up in everyday language, proving that sometimes words, when put together, can make you “LOL” or “laugh out loud”!

        Technology has also transformed the words we use, into abbreviations and acronyms. Over the years, “electronic mails” became “e-mails”, and later “emails”. The growing popularity of such acronyms is giving rise to more and more acceptance into the language. The most widely referenced dictionary, the Oxford English Dictionary (OED), has lately included acronyms

like “FOMO”, “OMG”, “LOL” and many more in its corpus. This is hardly a surprise! As stated by David Crystal, honorary professor of linguistics at the University of Bangor, new colloquialisms spread like wildfire amongst groups on the internet. For example, “To Google” has become a universally understood verb. Also, the word “Facebook”, originally developed to be a social networking website, popularly gained acceptance as a noun, as well as a verb.

        According to an article published by the BBC News on 'How the Internet is changing Language', 10% of the words in an average text are not written in full. “We need evidence that people are using a word over a period of time. The group looks for evidence that a word has been in use for at least five years before it can earn its place in the dictionary”, said Fiona McPherson, senior editor in the new words group at the OED.

Google Trends provides statistics on search volume, as a proportion of the total search. The graph alongside shows some of the popular words across the internet and their relative occurrences in the last decade. According to the graph, “skype”, “sext”, “lol”, “hashtag” are the words used relatively more than the others in comparison. These, and the others presented in the bar graph, are included in the Oxford Dictionary, owing to their immense popularity on internet.
Over the last few years, most of the OED’s Word of the year declarations have been inspired by social media trends. “Selfie”, “vape”, “unfriend”, “youthquake” and “gif” are some of the social-media inspired words that have been chosen as words of the year by OED. Apart from these, in 2015, for the first time, the Oxford Dictionary declared a pictograph: “Face with tears of joy” emoji as its Word of the year. According to Oxford, this was chosen as the ‘word’ because it best reflected the ethos, mood, and preoccupations of 2015. The Oxford University Press had partnered with a leading mobile technology business- SwiftKey, to explore frequency and usage statistics for some of the most popular emojis across the world, and SwiftKey identified that made up 20% of all the emojis used in the UK in 2015, and 17% of those in the US: a sharp rise from 4% and 9% respectively in 2014. The word “emoji” has seen a similar surge: its usage more than tripled in 2015 over the previous year according to data from the Oxford Dictionaries Corpus. This led to the official inclusion of “selfie” in the OED in 2014. Other popular words when checked on different platforms, show trends that paved way for inclusion in the dictionary.

On a Concluding note...

New ways of communicating have emerged, owing to the trends popularized by social media. Facebook has done more than most platforms to offer up new meanings for common words such as unfriend, like, status, wall, page and profile. Twitter has changed the communications syntactically too! Owing to its initial 140-character limit, hashtags came into picture and have now spread to other social media platforms. They’ve even reached everyday speech, but hopefully spoofs such as Jimmy Fallon and Justin Timberlake’s sketch on 'The Tonight Show' will dissuade us from using them too frequently. But we will find hashtags all over popular culture, from greetings cards and t-shirts to the dialogue of sitcom characters. So, if we pay attention to discussions in our social networks, we can spot emerging new words, or who knows, maybe even coin one! Hope you “like” the idea!
